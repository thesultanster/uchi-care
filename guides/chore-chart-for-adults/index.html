What does a chore chart for adults actually look like?

A chore chart for adults isn’t a sticker grid — it’s an ownership map. Each recurring job gets one owner, a written standard, and a cadence; the "chart" is just the visible record of who carries what. If your chart needs a parent to enforce it, it’s a kids’ chart with adult names on it.

Why fridge charts and spreadsheets die by week 3

Paper charts and Notion boards fail the same way: someone has to maintain them, and maintaining the chart becomes its own unowned chore. They also track only the doing — not the noticing or planning — so the person who updates the chart is quietly doing the mental load for both of you.

The adult version: cards, owners, receipts

Uchi replaces the grid with a deck of manga trading cards — one per job, one owner per card, the standard written on the card itself. Weekly and monthly quests roll over automatically (no one has to reset the chart), clears get stamped 済, and the Review tab is the chart’s history: who did what, every week, no arguing with the record.

Does gamification work for adults?

Points-for-rewards doesn’t — adults don’t need a gold star for taking out the bins. What works is game structure: clear rules, visible state, and a little mischief. Uchi’s steal mechanic (take credit for a card your partner let slip — 横取り!) turns enforcement into play instead of nagging.
