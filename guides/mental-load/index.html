What is the mental load, and how do you actually share it?

The mental load is the invisible third of every chore: noticing it needs doing, planning when and how, and remembering next time. You share it by transferring whole jobs — including the noticing — to one owner, not by asking your partner to "help more." Help still leaves you as the household’s project manager.

Why "just ask me to help" doesn’t work

If you have to ask, you’re still doing the job’s hardest part: tracking it. Delegating tasks one at a time means the delegator keeps the calendar, the standards, and the worry. That’s why the person "doing less" often genuinely believes things are equal — the visible half really is split. The invisible half isn’t.

Making invisible work visible

Write the noticing into the job. In Uchi every card spells out all three layers — 発見 spot it (notice before anyone asks), 作戦 sort it (timing, supplies, coordination), 撃破 do it — so "own the bathroom card" means own the whole bathroom job. When the full job is on the card, the mental load finally shows up where both of you can see it.

A 20-minute reset for your house

Sit down together, deal out the deck, and put a name on every card. The rule: the owner carries spot-sort-do alone, to the agreed standard, and nobody nags. Trade until it feels fair, then let the weekly quests and receipts do the remembering. Most couples find 3–4 cards were silently owned by one person — that’s the mental load, made visible.
